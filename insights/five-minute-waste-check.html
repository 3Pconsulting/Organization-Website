Owner Exercise

The 5-Minute Waste Check

You do not need a full consulting project to notice where work is breaking down. Start with one recurring task and examine what actually happens between request and completion.

5-minute exercise · Practical guidance for owner-led businesses

Choose one task that repeats.

Do not select “sales,” “operations,” or “customer service.” Select one specific recurring workflow such as answering a new inquiry, preparing a quote, scheduling a job, ordering supplies, completing a repair, or sending an invoice.

The goal is not to redesign everything. The goal is to expose one source of repeated waste clearly enough to take action.

Minute 1: Write the real steps.

List what people actually do—not what the policy says should happen. Include text messages, handwritten notes, owner approvals, duplicate entries, phone calls, and personal reminders.

Minute 2: Mark every wait.

Put a “W” beside every point where work sits because someone is unavailable, information is missing, approval is required, or the next owner is unclear.

Common waits

Waiting for pricing, customer details, scheduling confirmation, a supervisor’s answer, inventory information, payment, or someone to remember the next step.

Minute 3: Mark every repeat or correction.

Put an “R” beside information entered more than once, work returned for correction, calls repeated because no update was provided, and steps performed again because the result was not visible.

Minute 4: Mark every owner dependency.

Put an “O” beside every step that must return to the owner—not because the decision is consequential, but because the process, authority, information, or standard is missing.

Minute 5: Choose one control.

Do not solve all marked problems at once. Choose the point creating the most repeated delay or interruption and test one control.

Missing information

Add a required field, intake form, checklist, or standard question.

Unclear ownership

Assign one person responsible for moving the item to the next stage.

Forgotten follow-up

Add a status, due date, trigger, or scheduled reminder visible to the team.

Repeated owner questions

Define a decision rule, threshold, example, or escalation point.

Duplicate entry

Select one source of truth and stop maintaining an unnecessary copy.

Measure the test for one week.

Track one simple result: fewer interruptions, faster response, fewer corrections, fewer open items, faster invoicing, or clearer status. A change is only useful when it improves the work rather than merely adding another step.
